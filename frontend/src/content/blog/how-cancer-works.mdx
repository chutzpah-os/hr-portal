Cancer kills more than 10 million people per year. Most of us have lost someone to it. Most of us still don't fully understand what it actually is.

This article is not about hope or awareness. It is an explanation. What cancer is, how it starts, why it spreads, and why it has proven so difficult to stop.

---

## What Is Cancer, Exactly?

Your body is made of approximately 37 trillion cells. Each cell contains your DNA — a complete instruction manual for building and running a human being. Under normal conditions, cells follow those instructions precisely: they grow, divide when needed, carry out their function, and die on schedule. This process is called the cell cycle, and it is tightly regulated.

Cancer begins when something disrupts those instructions.

Specifically, cancer starts with mutations — errors in the DNA of a single cell. These errors can be caused by:

- **Carcinogens** — substances that damage DNA: tobacco smoke, UV radiation, certain chemicals, alcohol
- **Viruses** — some viruses (HPV, Hepatitis B, Hepatitis C) insert their own genetic material into host cells, disrupting normal function
- **Inherited mutations** — some people are born with genetic variants that significantly increase risk (BRCA1/BRCA2 for breast cancer, for instance)
- **Random errors** — DNA replication is not perfect. Over billions of cell divisions across a lifetime, mistakes accumulate even in the absence of external triggers

A single mutation is usually not enough. The body has multiple safeguard systems. The problem is accumulation: when enough mutations hit the right genes, those safeguards fail.

---

## The Genes That Get Damaged

Two categories of genes are central to cancer:

**Oncogenes** are genes that normally promote cell growth and division. Think of them as the accelerator in a car. When a mutation permanently activates an oncogene, the cell receives a constant "grow and divide" signal — regardless of what the body actually needs. The accelerator gets stuck.

**Tumor suppressor genes** are genes that slow down cell division, repair DNA damage, or trigger programmed cell death (apoptosis) when a cell is too damaged to save. These are the brakes. When mutations disable tumor suppressor genes, those braking mechanisms fail.

Cancer typically requires damage to both systems — an accelerator stuck on and brakes that no longer work. One of the most well-known tumor suppressor genes is **TP53**, which encodes a protein called p53, often called "the guardian of the genome." Mutations in TP53 are found in more than half of all human cancers.

## How a Tumor Forms

Once a cell has lost enough of its regulatory control, it begins dividing uncontrollably. Its daughter cells inherit the same mutations and divide again. Over time — often years — this creates a mass of abnormal cells: a **tumor**.

Not all tumors are cancerous. A **benign** tumor grows locally, does not invade surrounding tissue, and does not spread. A **malignant** tumor — cancer — can do both.

Malignant tumors develop their own blood supply (a process called angiogenesis), allowing them to grow beyond the size that diffusion of oxygen alone would permit. They also evolve. Because cancer cells divide rapidly and with poor quality control, new mutations accumulate quickly. The tumor becomes genetically diverse — a moving target.

---

## Metastasis: When Cancer Spreads

The most dangerous phase of cancer is **metastasis**: the process by which cancer cells leave the original tumor, travel through the bloodstream or lymphatic system, and establish new tumors in distant organs.

Not all cancers metastasize at the same rate or to the same places. Breast cancer tends to spread to the bones, lungs, liver, and brain. Prostate cancer tends to go to the bones. Colorectal cancer commonly spreads to the liver.

Metastatic cancer is dramatically harder to treat than localized cancer. Surgery can remove a tumor. Removing metastases across multiple organs simultaneously is, in most cases, impossible.

This is why early detection saves lives. A cancer caught before it has spread has a fundamentally different prognosis than one caught after.

## The Main Types

Cancer is not one disease. It is more than 100 distinct diseases, classified by the type of cell where they originate:

- **Carcinomas** — arise from epithelial cells (skin, and the lining of organs and glands). The most common type: lung, breast, prostate, and colorectal cancers are all carcinomas.
- **Sarcomas** — arise from connective tissue: bone, muscle, fat, cartilage. Less common, but often aggressive.
- **Leukemias** — cancers of blood-forming tissue (bone marrow). They do not form solid tumors but flood the bloodstream with abnormal white blood cells.
- **Lymphomas** — cancers of the lymphatic system. Divided into Hodgkin and non-Hodgkin lymphoma.
- **CNS cancers** — cancers of the brain and spinal cord. Among the most difficult to treat due to the blood-brain barrier and the precision required in treatment.

Each type — and in many cases each subtype — behaves differently, responds to different treatments, and has a different prognosis. A "cancer diagnosis" without knowing the type and stage is nearly meaningless from a clinical standpoint.

## Why It Is So Hard to Cure

Cancer is hard to eliminate for several interconnected reasons.

**It originates from your own cells.** The body's immune system is trained to distinguish "self" from "non-self" and attack non-self. Cancer cells are self — modified self, but self. Identifying and attacking them without destroying healthy tissue is genuinely difficult.

**It mutates continuously.** A tumor is not a fixed entity. It evolves. When a treatment kills most of the cancer cells, the cells that survive are the ones most resistant to that treatment. Under selection pressure, cancers develop resistance — to chemotherapy, to targeted therapies, sometimes to immunotherapy. This is the same mechanism as antibiotic resistance, playing out inside a patient.

**It is genetically heterogeneous.** Even within a single tumor, different cells carry different mutations. A therapy that targets one genetic profile may be ineffective against cells with a different profile in the same mass.

**Metastatic cells are difficult to detect and eliminate.** By the time cancer has spread, the battle is being fought on multiple fronts simultaneously.

## How Treatment Works Today

Modern cancer treatment uses several approaches, often in combination:

**Surgery** — physical removal of the tumor. Effective for localized, accessible cancers. Does not address cells that have already spread.

**Radiation therapy** — high-energy radiation damages DNA inside cancer cells, killing them or preventing division. Targeted to specific areas. Normal cells nearby are also affected, which is why radiation has side effects.

**Chemotherapy** — drugs that kill rapidly dividing cells. Effective against many cancers, but also damages healthy rapidly-dividing cells (hair follicles, gut lining, bone marrow), explaining the characteristic side effects: hair loss, nausea, immune suppression.

**Targeted therapy** — drugs designed to block specific molecular targets involved in cancer growth (e.g., a specific mutated protein). More precise than chemotherapy. The challenge: cancers often develop resistance.

**Immunotherapy** — approaches that help the immune system recognize and attack cancer cells. Checkpoint inhibitors (such as anti-PD-1 antibodies) have produced dramatic results in certain cancers that were previously considered untreatable. Not universally effective.

**CAR-T cell therapy** — a patient's own T cells are extracted, genetically engineered to recognize cancer cells, and reinfused. Highly effective in certain blood cancers. Expensive and complex.

**Hormone therapy** — for cancers that are driven by hormones (breast, prostate), blocking hormone production or action can slow or stop tumor growth.

## Where Research Is Focused

The frontier of cancer research today concentrates on several areas:

- **Liquid biopsies** — detecting cancer DNA fragments circulating in the bloodstream, enabling earlier detection before a tumor is large enough to find by imaging
- **Combination immunotherapy** — pairing multiple immune-activating agents to overcome resistance
- **mRNA therapeutics** — the same technology underlying COVID-19 vaccines, adapted to teach the immune system to recognize specific tumor antigens
- **Artificial intelligence** — training models to detect cancers in imaging and pathology slides earlier than human clinicians, and to predict treatment response based on genomic profiles

None of these are a cure. All of them represent incremental progress toward better outcomes for more patients.

---

Cancer is not one enemy. It is millions of versions of your own cells that have stopped following the rules, accumulating errors, evading detection, and evolving against every attempt to stop them. That is why defeating it is one of the hardest problems in science. And why the research continues.
