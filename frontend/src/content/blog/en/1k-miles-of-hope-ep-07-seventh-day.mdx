I decide to run 1,000 miles over 100 days against cancer.

Day seven. Zero miles.

I spend the entire day on the couch with an ice pack on the left ankle. I keep switching it out — ice, wait, ice again. I check the ankle every few hours looking for some sign that things are moving in the right direction. Less swelling. Less pain on contact. Anything.

Nothing changes.

There's a specific kind of patience that this kind of day demands — not the patience of someone who has accepted the situation, but the patience of someone who is still fighting it, hour by hour, from a couch. I'm not resting. I'm waiting. Those are different things.

I wanted to run today. I had it planned. I lay there watching the hours go by knowing every one of them is a mile I'm not covering.

The ankle doesn't respond.

Tomorrow I try a different approach. I can't keep doing the same thing and expecting different results. Something has to change — maybe the treatment, maybe the technique, maybe both.
