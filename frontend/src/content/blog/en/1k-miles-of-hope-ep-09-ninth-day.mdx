I decide to run 1,000 miles over 100 days against cancer.

Day nine. The avenue is empty. Midnight.

---

Around the seventh kilometer, the ankle sends a signal. Not a scream — a knock. I slow down. My stride gets smaller, choppier. I've never looked graceful running. I look like a waddling duck trying to take flight. If the ankle tightens tonight, tomorrow I won't be able to walk — I know that already.

I keep going. Slower.

The moon hangs low. I look up for a second and it's just there, enormous, as if it showed up specifically to be noticed.

---

Earlier in the day I had seen a news story. A man — no older than fifty — had died of advanced stomach cancer. He was known as someone who was good to everyone. Months before he died, he had done something unusual: he called together his family and friends for a celebration. Not a vigil, not a farewell dinner — something he called a *living wake*. He stood in the middle of the room, alive, surrounded by the people who loved him, and he smiled.

The goal wasn't to deny death. It was to refuse to let death have the last word.

I watched the video in the morning. He didn't look like a man saying goodbye. He looked like a man who had finally understood something.

---

That image stayed with me on the avenue.

It made me think of Pau Donés.

Spanish singer, songwriter, frontman of the band Jarabe de Palo. In August 2015, at 48, he started feeling sharp abdominal pain. Within weeks he had a diagnosis: colon cancer. Emergency surgery. More than thirty concerts cancelled. Then chemotherapy. Then, in 2016, no detectable signs of the disease. Then, in 2017, a relapse — small tumors in the peritoneum. He lived with cancer for five more years after that.

What set Pau apart wasn't that he fought cancer. He said he didn't want to fight it. He wanted to live alongside it. He said:

*"Cancer made me very happy, because I was losing many things in life."*

Before the diagnosis, he lived on planes, in buses, between sound checks. The cancer stopped everything. He spent time with his daughter. He paid attention to things he'd been skipping past for years. He kept composing, kept recording, kept performing when his body allowed it. In 2020, knowing the disease had advanced, he released one last album and a song many heard as his farewell — *Eso que tú me das*.

He died on June 9, 2020. Fifty-three years old.

He and the man from the news had never met. But they had found the same thing: that a diagnosis isn't a full stop. It's more like a change in rhythm.

---

The ankle loosens somewhere around kilometer eleven.

I pick up the pace.

---

Then: a white Fiat Argo, two men inside, moving slow on the avenue.

It passes me. Keeps going.

Then it comes back.

I track it from the corner of my eye. It goes by a second time. Then a third. Fourth. On the fifth pass it pulls over, signals, and sits there honking. Stopped. Engine running.

I run through the options fast. A friend who recognized me? No friend of mine does that at midnight. And I'm not famous enough on social media for a stranger to. The charitable reading didn't last long. The two remaining options were both worse — one bad, one a lot worse.

I don't stop.

I run faster.

The car eventually disappears. I'm heading home.

I thought about Pau Donés and the man with the living wake for most of those 21 km. Two people who, facing the one thing no one outruns, decided not to wait for better conditions.

I don't know what they felt on the inside. I only know what they showed.
